# PawShelter: A Human Factors Engineering Approach to Animal Shelter Management Dashboard Design

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Overview of the Assignment Objective

This project aims to develop a comprehensive dashboard interface for animal shelter management systems, applying fundamental principles of Human Factors Engineering (HFE) to create an intuitive, efficient, and user-friendly platform. The assignment required the design and implementation of a web-based dashboard that supports shelter operations by integrating multiple functionalities including animal tracking, health monitoring, feeding schedules, and inventory management into a single, cohesive interface.

### 1.2 Purpose of the Project: Designing a User-Friendly Dashboard for Animal Shelter Management

The primary purpose of this project is to design and implement a user-friendly dashboard for animal shelter management that addresses the complex operational needs of modern animal care facilities. The dashboard, named "PawShelter," serves as a centralized command center that enables shelter staff to monitor, track, and manage various aspects of animal care efficiently. The system consolidates critical information from multiple operational areas including animal health status, feeding schedules, real-time location tracking, environmental conditions, inventory levels, and alert notifications, presenting them in an organized and accessible format. By providing a single point of access to all essential information, the dashboard reduces the cognitive burden on staff members who previously had to navigate between different systems or rely on manual record-keeping methods, ultimately improving the quality of care provided to shelter animals.

### 1.3 Relevance of Human Factors Engineering (HFE) in Dashboard Design

Human Factors Engineering principles are fundamental to the successful design of this dashboard because they directly address how humans interact with complex information systems. The field of HFE focuses on optimizing the interaction between humans and technology by understanding human capabilities, limitations, and cognitive processes. In the context of animal shelter management, where critical decisions must be made quickly and accurately under potentially stressful conditions, applying HFE principles ensures that the interface supports rather than hinders human performance. The dashboard design incorporates key HFE concepts including cognitive load reduction through information hierarchy, recognition over recall through consistent visual design patterns, error prevention through clear feedback mechanisms, and accessibility considerations to accommodate users with varying levels of technical expertise. These principles are particularly important in shelter environments where staff members may have diverse educational backgrounds, varying levels of computer literacy, and must often multitask while caring for multiple animals simultaneously.

### 1.4 Target Users: Shelter Staff, Volunteers, Visitors with Different Levels of Technology Literacy

The PawShelter dashboard is designed to accommodate a diverse range of users with varying technical competencies and roles within the animal shelter environment. The primary target users include full-time shelter staff members who require comprehensive access to all system features for daily operations, part-time volunteers who may have limited experience with complex software systems, and occasional visitors such as veterinary professionals or shelter administrators who need quick access to specific information. These user groups differ significantly in their technology literacy levels, frequency of system use, and depth of interaction with various features. Full-time staff members typically require advanced functionality and may develop proficiency through regular use, while volunteers need a more intuitive interface that allows them to contribute effectively with minimal training. The design addresses these differences through progressive disclosure of information, consistent navigation patterns, extensive use of visual cues including icons and color-coding, and context-sensitive help features that support users at all skill levels without overwhelming those with less technical experience.

## 2. Background / Problem Statement

Animal shelters face numerous challenges in managing their daily operations effectively, particularly as the number of animals under their care increases and the complexity of care requirements grows. Traditional management approaches that rely heavily on paper-based records, manual tracking systems, and fragmented digital tools create significant operational inefficiencies and increase the likelihood of errors that can negatively impact animal welfare. Shelter staff must simultaneously monitor the health status of multiple animals, maintain feeding schedules that vary by species and individual dietary needs, track animal locations within facility grounds, manage inventory levels for food and medical supplies, and respond promptly to health alerts or behavioral concerns. The absence of a unified system forces staff members to mentally integrate information from multiple sources, increasing cognitive load and creating opportunities for oversight or misinterpretation.

Existing shelter management systems often suffer from significant limitations that compromise their effectiveness and user adoption. Many available solutions provide overly complex interfaces with dense information displays that overwhelm users, particularly those with limited technical training. These systems frequently require extensive training periods and ongoing reference to documentation, which places excessive demands on human memory and recall capabilities. Additionally, traditional systems may lack real-time updating capabilities, forcing staff to manually refresh data or work with potentially outdated information when making time-sensitive decisions. Some existing solutions fail to provide adequate visual feedback for critical status indicators, requiring users to interpret numerical data or text-based alerts rather than leveraging the human capacity for rapid visual pattern recognition. Furthermore, many current systems do not adequately address the diverse needs of different user roles, creating interfaces that are either too simplistic for experienced staff members or too complex for volunteers or temporary personnel.

The importance of a cognitively efficient, intuitive dashboard in reducing errors and improving decision-making cannot be overstated in the context of animal shelter operations. When staff members are required to process information from multiple sources under time pressure, the resulting cognitive overload increases the likelihood of missed alerts, scheduling errors, or delayed responses to health concerns. A well-designed dashboard that presents information in accordance with human cognitive capabilities enables rapid situation assessment, supports pattern recognition for identifying trends or anomalies, and reduces the mental effort required to access critical information. By minimizing cognitive load, the dashboard allows staff members to focus their mental resources on decision-making and problem-solving rather than on navigating complex interface elements or searching for necessary information. This cognitive efficiency directly translates to improved response times for health emergencies, more accurate feeding schedule adherence, better inventory management, and ultimately enhanced animal welfare outcomes.

## 3. Objectives

### 3.1 Design an Intuitive and Efficient Dashboard Interface

The primary objective is to create a dashboard interface that enables users to accomplish their tasks with minimal cognitive effort and maximum efficiency. This requires careful attention to information architecture, visual hierarchy, and interaction design principles that align with human perceptual and cognitive capabilities. The interface must present complex information in a format that allows users to quickly understand the current state of all animals and facilities, identify areas requiring attention, and access detailed information when needed. Efficiency is achieved through logical grouping of related functions, minimizing the number of clicks required to access frequently used features, and providing multiple pathways to important information to accommodate different user preferences and workflows.

### 3.2 Support Users' Decision-Making and Minimize Human Error

The dashboard must actively support decision-making processes by presenting information in a format that facilitates evaluation, comparison, and action planning. Critical information such as health alerts, feeding schedules, and inventory levels must be prominently displayed and clearly prioritized according to urgency and importance. Error prevention is addressed through visual confirmation of actions, clear labeling of all interactive elements, confirmation prompts for irreversible operations, and immediate feedback for all user interactions. The system must also help users avoid errors of omission by automatically surfacing critical information through alert systems, reminders, and status indicators that require minimal interpretation.

### 3.3 Apply HFE Principles: Cognition, Memory, and Training

The dashboard design systematically applies core HFE principles related to human cognition, memory, and training requirements. Cognitive principles are implemented through information chunking, progressive disclosure of complex data, use of visual metaphors and icons to reduce processing time, and consistent design patterns that allow users to apply learned behaviors across different sections of the interface. Memory support is provided through consistent menu placement, persistent navigation elements, visual cues that aid recognition rather than requiring recall, and contextual information displays that eliminate the need to remember details from previous screens. Training requirements are minimized through intuitive design, extensive use of familiar visual conventions, built-in help systems, and interface elements that guide users through complex tasks without requiring extensive documentation or external training materials.

### 3.4 Ensure Accessibility for Users with Different Technology Literacy Levels

The interface must be accessible and usable by individuals with varying levels of computer experience and technical knowledge. This objective requires careful consideration of interface complexity, language clarity, visual design clarity, and support mechanisms. The design accommodates novice users through simplified default views, prominent help options, clear visual feedback for all actions, and the avoidance of technical jargon in favor of plain language labels. At the same time, the interface must not frustrate experienced users by providing advanced features, keyboard shortcuts, and efficient workflows that support power users. The balance between simplicity and functionality is achieved through progressive disclosure, where basic functions are immediately accessible while advanced features remain available for those who need them.

## 4. Project Scope

### 4.1 Included Features

#### Dashboard Overview with Comprehensive Analytics

The main dashboard provides a centralized overview of all shelter operations with real-time statistics displayed in visually prominent stat cards. The dashboard features four primary metric cards showing total animals across all species, animals requiring attention (with health warnings or alerts), pending feeding tasks scheduled for the day, and active tracking devices currently online. A critical alert banner spans the full width at the top of the information area, displaying the most urgent alert that requires immediate action, with dismiss and action buttons for quick response. The dashboard includes multiple information cards for upcoming feedings with countdown timers, low stock inventory items, and a live mini-map showing current animal locations. Four comprehensive charts provide analytical insights: species distribution bar chart showing population counts across different species, health status doughnut chart displaying the proportion of animals in good, warning, and danger health states, feeding progress chart tracking daily schedule completion rates, and alert distribution chart showing alert counts by severity level. An activity timeline displays recent shelter activities chronologically, and an environmental status summary shows the condition of all monitored enclosures.

#### Animal Information Management and Tracking

The Animals Management section provides comprehensive tracking capabilities for all shelter animals, supporting six species: goats, sugar gliders, alligators, snails, porcupines, and red foxes. Each animal record includes detailed information including name, species identification, photographs for visual recognition, and health status classification (good, warning, danger) displayed through color-coded badges. The system tracks species-specific health metrics such as heart rate, body temperature, activity levels, weight, air quality, and specialized parameters including basking temperature and water conditions for alligators, humidity levels for snails and sugar gliders, and perimeter security status for foxes. Each animal card displays the last-fed timestamp, health status indicator, and device connection icons showing which monitoring systems are active (heart rate sensors, temperature sensors, CCTV cameras, GPS tracking devices, and safety locks). Clicking on an animal card opens a detailed modal showing comprehensive tracking information including current location, movement status (moving or resting), battery level of tracking devices, last charging time, complete health metrics grid, recent activity notes, and active alerts with detailed information and action buttons.

#### Live Animal Tracking with Interactive Map

The Live Tracking page provides real-time GPS-based location monitoring for animals equipped with tracking devices. The system displays tracked animals on an interactive map using Leaflet.js, with species-specific color coding and emoji icons for each animal marker. The interface includes species filter buttons that allow users to show or hide specific animal groups, with live counts displayed for each species. A quick-zoom dropdown enables rapid navigation to species-specific clusters or the entire facility view. Movement trail visualization can be toggled on or off, displaying historical movement paths as colored polylines on the map when enabled. The tracking interface shows real-time statistics including the number of tracked animals, count of moving versus resting animals, active alerts among tracked animals, and filter coverage percentage. Each animal marker includes visual indicators for low battery status and active alerts, and clicking on markers opens detailed animal information modals. A sidebar list displays all tracked animals with their current locations, movement status, and last-fed information, providing an alternative view format to complement the map display.

#### Comprehensive Alert and Notification System

The Alerts page provides a centralized alert management interface with sophisticated filtering and prioritization capabilities. Alerts are automatically generated from multiple sources including abnormal health metrics, feeding schedule issues, inventory shortages, environmental condition deviations, and system status changes. The alert system uses a three-tier severity classification (urgent, moderate, routine) with distinct visual styling, icons, and progress indicators that communicate both the nature and urgency of each alert. The interface includes a toolbar with severity filter buttons that allow staff to focus on specific alert types, with live counts displayed for each severity level. An alert summary section displays key statistics including total open alerts, severity distribution breakdown, and active filter status. Each alert card provides detailed information including the alert message, affected animal name and species, location, health status, timestamp, and recommended actions. Staff can acknowledge alerts directly from the interface, which transitions them to a resolved state while maintaining historical records. Critical alerts also appear as dismissible banner notifications throughout the dashboard to ensure time-sensitive issues receive immediate attention.

#### Advanced Feeding Schedule Management

The Feeding Schedule page provides comprehensive management capabilities for both manual and automated feeding operations. The interface is organized by species with tabbed navigation allowing staff to view and manage feeding configurations for each animal group separately. Each species view displays three main sections: an auto-feeder control card with an on/off toggle switch and current meal meter showing daily food intake progress, a portion control card displaying recommended daily intake, current portions dispensed, and controls for adjusting feeding parameters, and an auto-feeder schedule card showing all scheduled feeding times with editable time slots and portion amounts. The system tracks feeding history with detailed logs showing date, time, portions dispensed, grams of food, and feeding method (auto feeder or manual). Staff can edit portion control settings through a modal interface that allows adjustment of feeding parameters, and feeding schedules can be modified when the auto-feeder is enabled. The interface includes visual progress indicators showing daily food intake against recommended amounts, helping staff ensure adequate nutrition for each species. Manual feeding can be triggered directly from the interface, which updates animal records and feeding history automatically.

#### Health Monitoring and Medical Records

The Health Monitor page provides dedicated tools for tracking animal health and medical care schedules. The interface displays vaccination schedules showing upcoming vaccinations by animal group, due dates, and status indicators (upcoming, active, completed). The system tracks medication schedules including antibiotic courses, dosage frequencies, and treatment durations. Health status is continuously monitored through sensor data integrated from individual animal records, and the interface provides access to detailed health metrics including vital signs, temperature readings, activity levels, and species-specific health parameters. The health monitoring system supports proactive care by displaying scheduled medical procedures and treatment plans, ensuring that preventative care such as vaccinations are completed on schedule and that ongoing treatments such as medication courses are properly administered.

#### Cage Monitor with Live CCTV and Environmental Control

The Cage Monitor page provides real-time video monitoring and environmental control capabilities for all animal enclosures. The interface features a live CCTV feed display with video playback for each species enclosure, switchable through tabbed navigation. Camera controls enable staff to pan the camera view in four directions (up, down, left, right) and center the view, with visual indicators showing the current camera position. The system includes recording functionality with start/stop controls and a visual recording banner, as well as screenshot capture capabilities with confirmation notifications. Each enclosure's environmental conditions are displayed in a dedicated section showing temperature, humidity, and air quality metrics with optimal range indicators and current status classifications (optimal, warning, critical). The environmental controller section provides direct control over enclosure systems including lighting controls with adjustable brightness sliders and preset modes (night, day, UVB), ventilation controls with speed settings (low, medium, high) and automatic ventilation toggle, and cage lock controls for secure enclosures. All environmental parameters are displayed with real-time values, target ranges, and status indicators that help staff maintain optimal conditions for each species.

#### Inventory Management with Shopping List Integration

The Inventory Management page provides comprehensive supply tracking and procurement support. The interface displays all inventory items in a grid layout with visual icons, category classifications (Feed, Health, Equipment), current stock levels, and minimum threshold indicators. Items below minimum stock levels are visually highlighted to draw immediate attention to shortages. Clicking on inventory items opens detailed modal views showing comprehensive information including item name, category, current stock level, minimum threshold, usage history, and related items. The system includes an integrated shopping list feature that allows staff to add low-stock or out-of-stock items directly to a procurement list. The shopping list can be viewed in a dedicated modal interface, cleared when no longer needed, and exported as a PDF document for procurement purposes. The shopping list automatically tracks item quantities and provides a summary count of items requiring purchase, streamlining the inventory restocking process.

#### Report Generation and Analytics

The Reports page provides comprehensive reporting capabilities for all aspects of shelter operations. The interface displays key performance statistics in stat cards showing feeding compliance percentage, overall health score, number of active CCTV cameras, and system uptime percentage. Six different report types can be generated: Health Reports providing comprehensive health status summaries for all animals with detailed metrics and trend analysis, Feeding Reports showing feeding schedule compliance, meal completion rates, and dietary statistics, Tracking Reports displaying animal movement patterns, location history, and tracking device status, Alerts Reports summarizing all alerts and notifications with severity distribution and resolution statistics, Inventory Reports showing stock levels, usage patterns, and procurement recommendations, and System Reports providing system performance metrics, device status overview, and operational statistics. Generated reports are stored locally and displayed in a recent reports list with generation timestamps, creator information, and action buttons for viewing or downloading. Reports can be viewed in detail or downloaded as text files for external use, supporting documentation and record-keeping requirements.

### 4.2 Excluded Features

#### Automated Animal Care Hardware Control

While the dashboard includes interfaces for monitoring and managing auto-feeder schedules and environmental control systems, the current scope explicitly excludes direct hardware control or integration with physical automated animal care devices such as robotic feeding mechanisms, automated medication dispensers, or autonomous health monitoring equipment. The system provides scheduling and configuration interfaces that would communicate with such devices, but does not include the low-level hardware control protocols, safety interlock systems, or regulatory compliance features required for direct device manipulation. This limitation maintains focus on the human-computer interaction aspects of the dashboard design while acknowledging that actual deployment would require integration with hardware control systems developed separately with appropriate safety and reliability considerations.

#### Integration with External Applications and Services

The system is designed as a self-contained management platform and does not include integration capabilities with external applications or services such as social media platforms for adoption promotion, third-party adoption websites, veterinary clinic record management systems, financial accounting software, or external inventory suppliers. While the dashboard can export data in standard formats (PDF, text files) that could be imported into external systems, it does not provide real-time data synchronization, API integrations, or bidirectional communication with external platforms. This scope limitation ensures that the dashboard remains focused on core shelter operations and maintains clear boundaries for system testing, maintenance, and user training without the added complexity of managing external dependencies, API versioning, cross-platform compatibility issues, or data privacy compliance across multiple integrated systems.

#### Real-Time IoT Device Integration

Although the dashboard displays sensor data and tracking information in real-time, the current implementation uses simulated data rather than true IoT device integration. The system does not include the communication protocols, data acquisition systems, or device management capabilities required for direct connection to physical sensors, GPS tracking devices, or environmental monitoring equipment. While the interface design supports and demonstrates the display of real-time information, actual deployment would require backend systems for device communication, data transmission protocols, network infrastructure, and device authentication that are beyond the scope of this dashboard-focused project. This limitation allows the project to focus on interface design and user experience without the technical complexity of hardware integration, but the design accommodates future integration through clear data models and display structures.

### 5.4 Perception

The dashboard leverages human perceptual capabilities to make information quickly understandable and actionable. Visual perception is optimized through strategic use of color, size, contrast, and spatial relationships. The glassmorphism design aesthetic creates depth perception that helps users distinguish between different layers of information hierarchy‚Äîbackground elements appear behind primary content, and interactive elements like buttons and cards appear elevated and prominent. Color perception is harnessed through semantic color-coding that aligns with universal associations: green for good/safe states, yellow/amber for warnings, and red for danger or urgent alerts. This color system is applied consistently across health status indicators, alert banners, environmental status tags, and progress indicators, enabling rapid visual scanning and pattern recognition without requiring detailed reading.\
Size and contrast are used strategically to guide attention: critical alerts appear in large, high-contrast banners at the top of the dashboard, stat cards use large, bold numbers with gradient text effects that draw the eye, and interactive elements like buttons and toggles have sufficient size and contrast to be easily distinguished from background elements. Iconography utilizes emoji symbols that are universally recognizable and culturally neutral, reducing the cognitive effort required to interpret visual cues. Spatial perception is enhanced through consistent grouping principles: related information appears together in cards or panels, filters and controls are grouped logically, and visual whitespace creates clear boundaries between different functional areas. The combination of these perceptual design elements enables users to quickly assess system states, identify areas requiring attention, and navigate the interface efficiently through visual scanning rather than detailed reading.

### 5.5 Attention Management

The dashboard design actively manages user attention to ensure critical information receives priority while preventing information overload. Visual hierarchy guides attention naturally from most important to least important information: the dashboard's critical alert banner appears at the top in red, drawing immediate attention to urgent issues; stat cards with large numbers and icons follow, providing quick status overview; and detailed information is positioned lower in the layout where users can access it when needed. Attention is directed through multiple visual cues including size, color, position, motion, and contrast. The notification badge in the header uses pulsing animation and a contrasting red background to attract attention when alerts are present, ensuring that users become aware of pending issues even when focused on other tasks.\
The alert system uses attention-grabbing techniques including prominent banner placement, red color coding for urgency, clear iconography, and dismissible action buttons that require explicit interaction, preventing important alerts from being overlooked. In the tracking interface, moving animals are distinguished from resting animals through status labels and visual indicators, helping users focus on animals that require active monitoring. The dashboard's auto-refresh capabilities update information every 30 seconds, ensuring that attention is maintained on current system states rather than stale data. Attention management also prevents overload through progressive disclosure: users see summary information first and can drill down for details when their attention is specifically directed, allowing them to control the amount of information processed at any given time. This approach balances the need to surface important information with the need to prevent cognitive overload.

### 5.6 Error Prevention and Feedback

The dashboard incorporates multiple mechanisms to prevent user errors and provide immediate feedback for all actions. Error prevention is achieved through constraints that prevent invalid actions: for example, the tracking filter system prevents users from hiding all species filters simultaneously, ensuring that at least one species remains visible on the map at all times. Similarly, the alert filter system maintains at least one severity level visible, preventing users from accidentally filtering out all alerts and missing critical information. Input validation prevents invalid data entry: environmental control sliders are constrained to valid ranges, portion control settings have minimum and maximum limits, and feeding schedule times must follow proper time format conventions.\
Feedback mechanisms provide immediate confirmation for all user actions, reducing uncertainty and preventing repeated actions. Every significant interaction triggers a toast notification that appears briefly, confirming what action was taken (e.g., "‚úÖ Jebat has been fed!", "üîÑ Reports list refreshed", "üìä Generating Health Report..."). Visual feedback is provided through state changes: buttons change appearance when clicked, toggles visually indicate their on/off state, filter buttons highlight when active, and loading states show progress indicators during report generation or data refresh operations. Hover states on interactive elements provide preview feedback, showing users what will happen when they click buttons or links. Error messages use clear, plain language to explain what went wrong and how to correct it, rather than technical error codes that would require interpretation or reference documentation. The combination of preventive constraints and immediate feedback creates a forgiving interface where errors are difficult to make and easily recoverable when they do occur.

### 5.7 Consistency and Standardization

Consistency is maintained across all pages and functional areas to create a predictable, learnable interface that reduces cognitive load and supports efficient navigation. Interaction consistency means that clicking, hovering, and scrolling behaviors work predictably across all pages, and common actions like closing modals, dismissing alerts, or navigating between pages follow the same patterns everywhere. This comprehensive consistency reduces the learning curve, prevents confusion, and enables users to transfer knowledge from one section of the system to another seamlessly.

### 6.2 Description of Layout and Functionalities

#### Navigation Bar and Sidebar

### 6.4 Justification of Design Decisions Based on HFE Principles

Design decisions throughout the PawShelter dashboard are systematically grounded in established HFE principles and cognitive science research, ensuring that every interface element serves both aesthetic and functional purposes in supporting human performance. The persistent sidebar navigation exemplifies the consistency principle (5.7), maintaining identical placement and structure across all pages to enable users to develop automatic navigation behaviors through spatial memory (5.2), reducing cognitive load (5.1) and eliminating the need to remember menu locations. The glassmorphism design aesthetic, while visually modern and engaging, serves the perception principle (5.4) by creating visual depth through layering and transparency, helping users distinguish between different levels of information hierarchy without requiring explicit borders or dividers that would add visual clutter.

The color-coding system for health status leverages multiple HFE principles simultaneously: perception (5.4) through rapid color recognition, attention management (5.5) by directing focus to urgent issues, and consistency (5.7) through universal semantic associations (red for danger, green for safety, yellow for warning). This multi-principle approach reduces the time required to assess animal conditions and minimizes errors (5.6) that might occur if status were communicated solely through text or numerical codes. The modal-based detail views for individual animals follow the memory support principle (5.2) by maintaining context through overlays rather than page replacements, allowing users to access detailed information without losing their place in the broader list view, which supports efficient information lookup and comparison tasks.

The grid-based layout for animal cards and inventory items utilizes the Gestalt principle of similarity and proximity within the cognition framework (5.1), creating visual groupings that facilitate scanning and pattern recognition. Users can quickly identify animals requiring attention through the visual prominence of alert badges and health status indicators, supporting both the attention management principle (5.5) for rapid situation assessment and the affordances principle (5.8) by making important information clearly visible. The progressive disclosure approach used throughout the interface, where summary information is presented first with details available on demand, directly addresses the cognitive limitation of working memory (5.1) by preventing information overload while maintaining access to comprehensive data when needed.

Error prevention mechanisms (5.6) are integrated through design constraints: filter systems prevent users from hiding all species or alert types simultaneously, ensuring critical information remains accessible. Feedback systems provide immediate confirmation through toast notifications for every significant action, supporting both error prevention and training (5.3) by reinforcing correct mental models. Visual affordances (5.8) make functionality discoverable: buttons appear raised and clickable, sliders clearly indicate draggability, and toggles resemble physical switches, reducing the need for instruction and supporting intuitive learning (5.3). The consistent application of these principles across all nine functional pages (Dashboard, Animals, Tracking, Alerts, Feeding, Health Monitor, Cage Monitor, Inventory, Reports) creates a cohesive system where design decisions build upon each other to support human performance comprehensively.

## 7. User Analysis

### 7.1 Target Users: Shelter Staff, Volunteers

The primary target users for the PawShelter dashboard are shelter staff members who work directly with animals on a daily or regular basis, and volunteers who contribute their time to support shelter operations. Shelter staff typically have more extensive training in animal care and may use the system as a primary tool for their work, requiring efficient access to comprehensive information and the ability to update records quickly. Volunteers often have varying levels of animal care experience and may require a more guided interface experience that helps them understand what information is important and how to take appropriate actions. Both user groups share the common goal of providing excellent care to animals, but they differ in their familiarity with the shelter's specific procedures, their frequency of system use, and their depth of technical expertise.

### 7.2 User Characteristics: Tech Literacy, Age Range, Experience with Animal Care

User characteristics vary significantly within the target user groups, requiring the interface to accommodate a wide range of abilities and preferences. Technology literacy levels range from basic computer users who are comfortable with common web applications to more advanced users who may prefer keyboard shortcuts and power-user features. Age ranges likely span from young adult volunteers in their twenties to experienced staff members who may be in their fifties or sixties, with different generational comfort levels with digital interfaces. Experience with animal care ranges from veterinary professionals with extensive medical knowledge to enthusiastic volunteers who are learning as they contribute, requiring the interface to provide appropriate levels of detail without overwhelming or underwhelming different user types.

The interface addresses these variations through adaptable complexity levels, clear visual communication that reduces reliance on technical knowledge, and support features that assist users at different skill levels. Font sizes are designed to be readable across age ranges, and the interface avoids reliance on color alone (important for color vision differences that become more common with age) by combining color coding with icons and text labels. The simplicity of the basic interface views supports less technically experienced users, while advanced features remain available for power users without complicating the primary experience.

### 7.3 Accessibility Considerations: Font Size, Color Blindness, Simplicity of Use

Accessibility considerations are integrated throughout the design to ensure the dashboard is usable by individuals with diverse abilities and needs. Font sizes are set to comfortable reading levels (typically 14-16px for body text) and scale appropriately for users who may require larger text. The interface uses high contrast ratios between text and background colors to support readability, and important information is never communicated through color alone. Instead, color coding is always accompanied by icons, text labels, or other visual indicators, ensuring that users with color vision deficiencies can access all information effectively.

The interface design prioritizes simplicity and clarity, reducing cognitive load for all users but particularly benefiting those who may experience cognitive fatigue or have learning differences. Interactive elements are large enough to be easily clicked or tapped, accommodating users with fine motor skill limitations. The consistent layout and navigation structure support users who may rely on screen readers or other assistive technologies, as the predictable structure enables efficient navigation through alternative input methods. Error messages and system feedback are provided in clear, plain language rather than technical terminology, ensuring that all users can understand system states and respond appropriately to interface prompts.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 How the Dashboard Supports All Applied HFE Principles

The PawShelter dashboard systematically supports human capabilities across all eight applied HFE principles, creating a comprehensive system that addresses cognitive, perceptual, attentional, and learning needs holistically. The interface reduces cognitive load (5.1) through information chunking, where related data is grouped together in cards or panels that can be processed as single units rather than requiring users to integrate information from multiple scattered locations. Visual hierarchy guides attention naturally to the most important information, and color-coding and iconography leverage rapid pattern recognition, enabling users to identify issues or status conditions at a glance. Progressive disclosure prevents cognitive overload by presenting summary information first with details available on demand, directly addressing working memory limitations.

Memory support (5.2) is provided through persistent navigation elements, consistent layout structures, and visual state indicators that eliminate the need to remember previous interactions or system states. The fixed sidebar navigation enables spatial memory development, while recent activity logs, timestamps, and visible configuration states externalize temporal information. Modal-based detail views maintain context by overlaying rather than replacing current views, reducing the memory load required for navigation. Training requirements (5.3) are minimized through intuitive design that leverages existing knowledge from common software applications, consistent design patterns that enable knowledge transfer across sections, and visual affordances that make functionality self-evident. Immediate feedback and toast notifications reinforce correct mental models during learning.

Perception (5.4) is optimized through strategic use of glassmorphism that creates depth perception, semantic color-coding that aligns with universal associations, and iconography that uses universally recognizable emoji symbols. Size, contrast, and spatial relationships are carefully managed to guide attention and enable rapid visual scanning. Attention management (5.5) directs focus through visual hierarchy, with critical alerts appearing prominently in red banners at the top, stat cards following with large numbers, and detailed information positioned lower. The notification badge uses pulsing animation to attract attention, and progressive disclosure allows users to control information density based on current focus needs.

Error prevention and feedback (5.6) mechanisms work together to create a forgiving interface: constraints prevent invalid actions (e.g., maintaining at least one filter active), input validation ensures data integrity, and immediate toast notifications confirm all actions. Visual feedback through state changes and hover states provides preview information, while clear error messages use plain language rather than technical codes. Consistency and standardization (5.7) are maintained across all nine functional pages through standardized visual elements, consistent interaction patterns, uniform terminology, and predictable layouts that create a stable framework users can rely on. This consistency multiplies the training investment by enabling knowledge transfer across all sections.

Affordances and visibility (5.8) make functionality discoverable through visual cues: buttons appear raised and clickable, sliders indicate draggability, and toggles resemble physical switches. Important information and controls maintain high visibility, with system status persistently displayed in headers and stat cards, critical alerts remaining visible until explicitly dismissed, and settings controls appearing directly on relevant pages rather than being hidden in menus. The combination of these eight principles creates a synergistic effect where each principle reinforces and amplifies the benefits of the others, resulting in a system that comprehensively supports human performance across all aspects of interaction with the dashboard.

The application of HFE principles throughout the dashboard design is expected to yield significant operational benefits for animal shelter management. ### 8.3 Limitations of the Current Design (e.g., Desktop-Only, No Real-Time IoT Integration)

The current dashboard design, while comprehensive in scope, includes several intentional limitations that define the project boundaries. The interface is optimized primarily for desktop and tablet devices, with responsive design that accommodates mobile viewing but does not provide the full feature set or optimal experience on small smartphone screens. This limitation acknowledges the complexity of the information being presented and the importance of adequate screen real estate for effective information visualization, though it may limit access for users who primarily work with mobile devices.

The system operates with simulated real-time data rather than true IoT device integration, meaning that sensor readings and tracking information are generated algorithmically rather than transmitted from physical monitoring devices. This limitation allows the project to focus on interface design and human-computer interaction principles without the additional complexity of hardware integration, data communication protocols, and device reliability concerns. While this approach demonstrates the interface's capabilities with realistic data, actual deployment would require integration with real sensor networks and tracking systems.

Additional limitations include the absence of multi-user collaboration features such as simultaneous editing, user-specific preferences and dashboards, or comprehensive audit trails of user actions. The system also does not include advanced features such as predictive analytics, machine learning-based health trend detection, or integration with external systems such as veterinary records databases or adoption platforms. These limitations reflect the project's focus on core dashboard functionality and HFE principles rather than attempting to address every possible feature that might be included in a comprehensive shelter management system.

### 9.2 Recommendations for Future Improvements

#### Mobile Version

Future development should prioritize the creation of a fully optimized mobile application version of the dashboard that provides core functionality for staff members who work primarily in the field or need to access information while away from desktop workstations. The mobile version should leverage mobile-specific interaction patterns such as swipe gestures, touch-optimized controls, and location-based features that take advantage of mobile device capabilities. The mobile interface should be designed as a companion to the desktop version rather than a simplified port, with features optimized for mobile use cases such as quick status checks, alert acknowledgment, and basic record updates that can be accomplished efficiently on smaller screens.

#### AI-Assisted Health Monitoring

The integration of artificial intelligence and machine learning capabilities would significantly enhance the dashboard's value by providing predictive health monitoring and automated pattern recognition. AI systems could analyze historical health data, feeding patterns, and behavioral indicators to identify animals at risk for health issues before symptoms become apparent, enabling proactive intervention. Machine learning algorithms could learn from staff responses to alerts to improve alert prioritization and reduce false positive notifications. Natural language processing could be applied to activity notes and veterinary records to extract meaningful insights and trends that might not be apparent through manual review. These AI enhancements would augment human decision-making rather than replacing it, presenting staff with AI-generated insights and recommendations while maintaining human oversight and control.

#### Integration with IoT Devices

The expansion of the dashboard to include full integration with Internet of Things devices would enable real-time monitoring capabilities that significantly enhance operational awareness and responsiveness. IoT integration would include sensor networks for environmental monitoring (temperature, humidity, air quality) that automatically update dashboard displays and generate alerts when conditions deviate from optimal ranges. GPS tracking devices on animals would provide true real-time location tracking rather than simulated movement patterns, enabling more accurate monitoring of animal locations and movement patterns. Automated feeding systems could integrate with the dashboard to confirm feeding completion automatically, reducing manual record-keeping and improving feeding schedule accuracy. Wearable health monitoring devices on animals could provide continuous vital sign monitoring with automatic alert generation for abnormal readings, enabling early detection of health issues that might otherwise go unnoticed between manual health checks.

These future improvements would build upon the solid HFE foundation established in the current dashboard design, ensuring that advanced capabilities are integrated in ways that continue to support human cognitive capabilities and minimize complexity for end users. The principles of clear information presentation, intuitive interaction design, and user-centered functionality should remain central to all future enhancements, ensuring that technological advances serve to enhance rather than complicate the user experience.
